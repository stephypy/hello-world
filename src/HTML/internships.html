About the EP Internship

The EP (Engineering Practicum) Internship was the freshman/sophomore summer internship program at Google; Google later changed the name to STEP (Student Training in Engineering Program). In this internship, students were paired with another fellow intern to work on a project together with the guidance of a host and a co-host for 12 weeks. In addition to working on a project, Google interns were encouraged to get a mentor, attend career-growth workshops, and interact with Google engineers outside of their team.

Application Process

Just a quick important note! The application process for (ST)EP interns and SWE interns is different; I will be covering what was my application process to apply to Google as a freshman.

I first applied to Google in fall 2018 through their website. Since I was a freshman at a time, the application for the EP internship required some short response essays with prompts about my motivation behind studying computer science, my extracurricular activities, etcetera. The application also required the standard personal information and resume, but as a freshman, the essays were going to be the most important factor for determining the opportunity to get an interview

After applying, I had to wait to either get an interview to move to the next phase, or to get rejected. I applied to the EP internship around September/October and I was invited for an interview in January. Generally, the technical interviews are held via phone call, but I was told I could do my interview in person at my school.

My interview consisted of two parts: a code reading and a whiteboard problem. During the code reading section of the interview, my interviewer gave me some coding to read and identify any errors or any changes that could improve the code. At the time I had not yet taken any data structures class, so ocassionally I had to ask my interviewer to explain the data structures I couldn't recognize. Overall, it was a learning experience for me because I walked out of the room having learned a new data structure. I was nervous at first, but the more I talked to my interviewer, the more I enjoyed the whole process.

Afterwards, I had my whiteboard technical interview. This interview was quite intimidating because my interviewer looked serious, but he was simply allowing me to explain my thought process. He gave me a problem and I started talking about the first solutions that came to mind. I asked for examples so I could identify any edge cases, and I did some drawings on the whiteboard to help me find a solution. My first implementation was not the most effective one and I acknowledged this observation to my interviewer. I had some time left, so I did some changes to my code to improve my solution. Sincerely, I walked out of that interview feeling defeated, but that feeling was a result of this being my first official whiteboard interview.

Several months later, I received a call back. Google seemed to like my performance and I was asked to fill out a form to either accept or reject the offer. After I accepted the offer, I entered the project matching phase. I was given a form to show my skills and interests and my recruiter set up interviews with potential hosts. I was matched with a team in Sunnyvale, California and I was going to meet my fellow intern during orientation.

My project

This internship was my first time doing frontend development. My project consisted on developing an internal tool (externally referred as Web Test Results Comparison) to help Google web developers troubleshoot their test results. This tool was developed using Angular with Typescript. It was estimated to improve the testing workflow of approximately 5k users. The project was significant because when testing, web developers have to take into account how their work might run in different platforms, browsers, and versions.

At the time, the Web Test Results Comparison only allowed showing one test run at a time which could be problematic since some tests can pass/fail on some configurations or runs. The goal for the summer was to implement a diffing algorithm to allow users to compare two test runs and improve the debugging process.

My main contributions related to the user inteface, namely creating a confirmation dialog for saving or canceling versions, creating the styling of buttons and icons, and highlighting differences found in two test log entries. Additional key contributions were adding routing to the diff results page, implementing a filter for user and test run status (such as failed, passed, etc), and I created test cases for the diffing algorithm which my co-worker implemented.

My thoughts

Being an intern for Google was both an intimidating and exciting experience, and although at times I felt like I didn't belong there (due to the imposter syndrome), I made some great friendships with other interns and I learned more about the world of web development thanks to the encouragement of my hosts. I was merely an intern who had just finished her first year in college and thus didn't have much experience, but after that summer I felt inspired to look more into what frontend development had to offer.
